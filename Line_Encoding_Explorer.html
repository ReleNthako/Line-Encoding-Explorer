Why do we even need alternatives to NRZ‑L?

NRZ‑L's problem is baseline wander and clock drift: if you send ten 1s in a row, the line just sits at HIGH with zero transitions. The receiver has no edges to resync its clock against, so it can lose count of exactly how many bits it received. Every other scheme here is really just a different strategy for guaranteeing enough transitions.

NRZ vs NRZ-I — what's the real difference in mindset?

NRZ‑L encodes the bit value directly as a voltage level. NRZ‑I encodes the bit as a decision to change or not change — it's differential. This matters practically: NRZ‑I doesn't care about your wiring polarity (flip the two wires and it still decodes correctly), while NRZ‑L breaks if the polarity is reversed.

Manchester — why the "double bandwidth" tradeoff is worth it

Because every single bit guarantees a mid-bit transition, the receiver can always resync its clock every bit period — that's what "self-clocking" means. This is why classic Ethernet (10BASE-T) used Manchester: reliability of timing was worth sacrificing raw bandwidth efficiency.

Bipolar AMI — the clever bit

AMI stands for Alternate Mark Inversion ("mark" = old telecom term for a 1). Because consecutive 1-pulses must alternate polarity, if a receiver ever sees two 1s in a row with the same polarity, it knows an error occurred — that's a free error-detection mechanism NRZ and Manchester don't get. It's also DC-balanced (average voltage ≈ 0), which single-ended NRZ isn't.
